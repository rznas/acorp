# Transformation Techniques

Three techniques from *INSPIRED* for moving an organization to modern, empowered, outcome-driven product practices. Use these when introducing discovery, piloting new ways of working, or transitioning away from roadmaps.

---

## Discovery Sprint (the GV 5-day technique)

A **discovery sprint** is a **one-week time box of product discovery work** designed to tackle a substantial problem or risk the team faces. (Some call it a "design sprint," but the work goes well beyond design, so "discovery sprint" is more accurate.) It's a great way to get value from the MVP concept and to give teams new to discovery a structured, proven recipe.

**Origin:** Google Ventures (GV) created a small team to help portfolio startups get their product efforts off to a strong start — spending a week rolling up sleeves and doing discovery side by side. Their method is captured in *Sprint: How to Solve Big Problems and Test New Ideas in Just Five Days* (Jake Knapp, John Zeratsky, Braden Kowitz).

**The five-day shape:**
1. Frame the problem — map the problem space, pick the problem to solve and the target customer.
2. Pursue several different approaches to the solution.
3. Narrow down and flesh out the potential solutions.
4. Build a single high-fidelity user prototype.
5. Put the prototype in front of real target users and observe their reactions.

Over the week the team explores dozens of ideas/approaches aimed at solving a significant business problem, always ending by validating the chosen solution with real users. The result is consistently big learning — the kind that can change the course of a product or company.

**When to run one:**
- The team has something big, critically important, and/or difficult to tackle.
- The team is just learning how to do product discovery.
- Things are moving too slowly and the team needs to recalibrate on how fast it can move.

### Discovery coaches

As teams adopted Agile, many hired Agile coaches — but most only know delivery/release, not discovery (more accurately *Agile delivery coaches*). A **discovery coach** is typically a former product manager or designer (or leader of those areas) from a leading product company, who works side by side with PMs and designers through real discovery cycles: ideation, prototyping, and validation with customers (value/usability), engineers (feasibility), and business stakeholders (viability). Effective discovery coaches must have first-hand product experience and must understand how to include engineering — being sensitive to their time while respecting their essential role in innovation. (Similar to Lean Startup coaches, who additionally help discover the business model and go-to-market; an effective one must still be strong at product.)

## Pilot Team Technique

The technology adoption curve applies to your *own organization* too: some people love change, some want to see someone else succeed first, some need time to digest, and a few hate change and will only move if forced. Rolling out a big change to everyone at once lets the laggards resist or even sabotage.

Instead, embrace it with a **pilot team**: roll the change out to a limited part of the organization first. Ask a product team to **volunteer** to try the new techniques and let them run for **a quarter or two** with the new way of working.

**Measuring it:** Compare the pilot team's effectiveness at delivering **business outcomes** — how well they hit their objectives vs. other teams or vs. their own past. Comparisons will be qualitative, but that doesn't make them less compelling.

**Outcomes:** If it goes well, you'll likely have other teams eager to adopt. If not, you may decide it isn't a fit, or adjust and retry.

**Maximizing success — choose the pilot carefully:**
- People who are **open to new ways of working**.
- Key team members are **co-located**.
- The team is **largely in control of how it works** and not heavily dependent on other teams still working the old way.

## Weaning an Organization Off Roadmaps

Many teams want to drop quarterly feature roadmaps but work in old-school orgs addicted to them. The transition technique:

**Keep your existing roadmap process for 6–12 months**, but **starting immediately**, every time you reference or discuss a roadmap item, **tag it with the actual business outcome** the feature is meant to drive.

Example: if you're adding PayPal as a payment method to increase conversion, always show the current conversion rate and the target you're hoping to reach. Most important, **after the feature ships, highlight the impact on that metric**:
- If the impact was good — celebrate it.
- If it wasn't — be explicit that you *did* ship the feature but the result wasn't successful; point out what was learned and that you have other ideas to get the desired result.

**The goal:** over time (up to a year), the organization shifts focus from *specific features launching on specific dates* to *business results*.

**Acknowledge why stakeholders love roadmaps** — and address both:
1. They want **visibility** into what you're working on and assurance you're working on the most important items.
2. They want to **plan the business** and need to know when critical things will happen.

The modern alternative addresses both: teams work on the prioritized **business objectives** set by leaders; **key results are shared transparently**; and the team makes **high-integrity commitments** when critical delivery dates are genuinely needed.
