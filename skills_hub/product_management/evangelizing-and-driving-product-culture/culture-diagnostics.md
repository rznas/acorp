# Culture Diagnostics

Tools from *INSPIRED* for diagnosing a product organization's health and assessing/establishing a strong product culture. Use the contrast list to spot anti-patterns, the loss-of-innovation and loss-of-velocity lists to find root causes of decline, and the culture dimensions to assess where you are and where you want to be.

> Cagan's core point: the difference between great product companies and the rest is mostly **culture**, not process. The loss-of-innovation list essentially *is* a culture-of-innovation checklist.

## Good Product Team vs. Bad Product Team

(Inspired by Ben Horowitz's "Good Product Manager/Bad Product Manager.") Use as a self-assessment — if many "bad" items hit close to home, raise the bar.

| Good team | Bad team |
|---|---|
| Pursues a compelling product vision with **missionary-like passion** | Are **mercenaries** |
| Gets ideas from vision/objectives, observing customer struggle, data, and new technology | **Gathers requirements** from sales and customers |
| Understands stakeholders' constraints; invents solutions that work for users **and** the business | **Gathers requirements** from stakeholders |
| Skilled at rapidly trying out ideas to find what's worth building | Holds meetings to generate **prioritized roadmaps** |
| Loves brainstorming with smart thought leaders across the company | Gets **offended** when outsiders suggest ideas |
| Product, design, engineering **sit side by side**; embrace the give-and-take | Sit in **silos**; require documents and scheduled meetings for requests |
| Constantly tries new ideas while protecting revenue and brand | Still **waiting for permission** to run a test |
| Insists on the skill sets needed (e.g., strong product design) | Don't even know what **product designers** are |
| Engineers try prototypes in discovery **daily** to make the product better | Show engineers prototypes at **sprint planning** just to estimate |
| Engages real users/customers **every week** | Thinks **they are the customer** |
| Knows many ideas won't work and good ones need several iterations | Just **builds what's on the roadmap**; satisfied with dates and quality |
| Knows speed comes from **right techniques**, not forced labor | Complains they're slow because colleagues aren't working hard enough |
| Makes **high-integrity commitments** after evaluating feasibility | Complains about being a "sales-driven company" |
| **Instruments** their work; adjusts based on data | Considers analytics/reporting a "nice to have" |
| **Integrates and releases continuously** (smaller, more stable releases) | Manual testing after a painful integration phase, then releases all at once |
| Obsesses over **reference customers** | Obsesses over **competitors** |
| Celebrates achieving **business impact** | Celebrates finally **releasing** something |

## Top 10 Reasons for Loss of Innovation

*Consistent innovation* = a team's ability to **repeatedly add value to the business**. Losing it is not inevitable (Amazon, Google, Facebook, Netflix are large *and* innovative). Orgs that lose it are missing one or more of:

1. **Customer-centric culture** — direct, frequent customer contact. (Bezos: customers are "beautifully, wonderfully dissatisfied"; the desire to delight them drives invention.)
2. **Compelling product vision** — at scale the original vision is often realized and founders (the vision-keepers) have moved on; the CEO or VP product must fill the void.
3. **Focused product strategy** — trying to please everyone at once is a sure path to failure; spell out an intentional sequence of target markets.
4. **Strong product managers** — a weak PM is a major cause of stalled innovation; at scale each team needs a strong, capable PM.
5. **Stable product teams** — innovation requires teams that have learned the space, technology, and customer pain; constant churn prevents this.
6. **Engineers in discovery** — include engineers from the very beginning (not just at the end) and expose them directly to customer pain.
7. **Corporate courage** — companies become risk-averse at scale, but the riskiest strategy is to stop taking risks; willingness to risk disrupting the current business is essential.
8. **Empowered product teams** — empowerment means teams solve assigned business problems however they see fit; handing out feature roadmaps forfeits the benefits.
9. **Product mindset** — product teams exist to *serve customers in ways that meet business needs*, not to *serve the business* (the IT mindset).
10. **Time to innovate** — teams consumed entirely by "keep the lights on" work (bugs, tech debt, one-off requests) can't innovate; ensure room for harder, higher-impact problems.

## Top 10 Reasons for Loss of Velocity

As orgs grow, things often slow down — they don't need to (the best accelerate). First places to look:

1. **Technical debt** — architecture that doesn't enable rapid evolution; attack it as an ongoing, concerted effort.
2. **Lack of strong product managers** — shows up visibly as a team of mercenaries; the PM hasn't inspired/evangelized, or the team lost confidence.
3. **Lack of delivery management** — the delivery manager removes impediments, which grow non-linearly with org size; they won't clear themselves.
4. **Infrequent release cycles** — release no less than every two weeks (great teams release multiple times/day); requires test and release automation.
5. **Lack of product vision and strategy** — teams need to see the big picture and how their work contributes.
6. **Lack of co-located, durable product teams** — split locations or outsourced engineers hurt both innovation and velocity; even communication breaks down.
7. **Not including engineers early enough in discovery** — engineers offer faster alternative approaches, but only if included from the start of ideation.
8. **Not using product design in discovery** — making designers work in parallel with engineers building slows things down and yields poor designs.
9. **Changing priorities** — rapidly shifting priorities cause churn, reduce throughput, and hurt morale.
10. **A consensus culture** — well-intentioned, but in practice decisions become very hard to make and everything slows to a crawl.

## Establishing a Strong Product Culture: Two Dimensions

Product culture has **two dimensions**:
- **Innovation** — can the company consistently come up with valuable solutions for customers? (This is product *discovery*.)
- **Execution** — can it deliver a productized, shippable version to customers? (This is product *delivery*.) Great ideas don't matter if you can't ship.

### Strong innovation culture
- **Experimentation** — teams know they can run tests; some succeed, many fail, and that's accepted.
- **Open minds** — good ideas can come from anywhere and aren't always obvious at the outset.
- **Empowerment** — individuals/teams feel empowered to try an idea.
- **Technology** — innovation can be inspired by new tech and data analysis, not just customers.
- **Business- and customer-savvy teams** — including developers — deeply understand business needs/constraints and have access to users/customers.
- **Skill-set and staff diversity** — different skills/backgrounds (especially engineering, design, product) drive innovative solutions.
- **Discovery techniques** — mechanisms exist to test ideas quickly and safely (protecting brand, revenue, customers, colleagues).

### Strong execution culture
- **Urgency** — people feel like it's wartime; moving slowly invites bad outcomes.
- **High-integrity commitments** — teams value commitments but insist they be high-integrity.
- **Empowerment** — teams have the tools, resources, and permission to meet commitments.
- **Accountability** — deep responsibility to meet commitments, with consequences (often reputational among peers).
- **Collaboration** — beyond autonomy, teams recognize the higher need to work together on the biggest objectives.
- **Results** — focus on results, not output.
- **Recognition** — teams take cues from what's rewarded; reward both the great new idea *and* delivering on a brutally tough commitment, and don't make missing commitments easily excusable.

### The tradeoff
Tough questions worth asking: Is an innovation culture inherently at odds with an execution culture? Does strong execution lead to a stressful environment? What kinds of people/leaders does each attract and need?

- **Few companies are strong at both.** **Amazon** is one of the best examples of strong innovation *and* execution — but its work environment "is not for the faint of heart." Companies exceptionally strong at execution tend to be tough places to work.
- Many are good at execution but weak at innovation; some are strong at innovation and only okay at execution; a depressing number are poor at both (older companies coasting on brand and customer base).

**The exercise:** Assess your team/company along both dimensions, then decide where you want — or need — to be.
