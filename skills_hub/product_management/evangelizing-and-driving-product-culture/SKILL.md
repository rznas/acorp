# Evangelizing and Driving Product Culture

Product success is ultimately a culture problem, not a process problem. This skill helps you sell the product dream, win and align stakeholders, and transform an organization toward an empowered, outcome-driven product culture. Source: Marty Cagan, *INSPIRED*.

## When to use this skill

- A PM/head of product/founder needs to inspire a team or "sell the dream"
- Stakeholders (execs, legal, finance, sales) have veto power and need alignment
- The org wants to communicate product learnings broadly
- You're driving transformation: introducing discovery, piloting new ways of working, or moving off roadmaps
- Innovation or delivery velocity is declining and you need to diagnose why
- You're assessing or trying to establish a strong product culture

## Product Evangelism: Selling the Dream

Evangelism is "selling the dream" (Guy Kawasaki) — helping people imagine the future and inspiring them to help create it. For founders, CEOs, and heads of product it's a huge part of the job; you can't assemble a strong team without it. For PMs (especially at large companies), weak evangelism means efforts get derailed before launch or wither after shipping. The goal: build a team of **missionaries, not mercenaries**. Responsibility falls primarily on the product manager.

The top-10 techniques to sell the dream:

1. **Use a prototype.** User stories make it hard to see the forest for the trees. A prototype lets people see both the forest *and* the trees — the big picture and how it hangs together.
2. **Share the pain.** Show the team the customer pain you're addressing. Bring engineers along on customer visits — many people must experience the pain themselves to get it.
3. **Share the vision.** Have a crisp product vision, strategy, and principles. Show how the work contributes to the vision and is true to the principles.
4. **Share learnings generously.** After every user test or customer visit, share what you learned — not just wins, but problems too. Give the team what they need to help solve it.
5. **Share credit generously; own failures.** Make the team view it as *their* product. When things go wrong, step forward, take responsibility for the miss, and show you're learning. They'll respect you for it.
6. **Master the demo.** A great demo is a *persuasive tool* — not training, not a user test. It shows the value of what you're building. Get really, really good at it (especially with customers and key execs).
7. **Do your homework.** People follow you when they believe you know what you're talking about. Be the undisputed expert on your users/customers and on your market (competitors, trends).
8. **Be genuinely excited.** If you're not excited about your product, fix that — change what you work on or change your role. Be sincere.
9. **Show enthusiasm.** Many PMs are genuinely excited but bad at showing it. This matters a lot. Be sincere, but let people see it — enthusiasm is contagious.
10. **Spend face time with your team.** If you're not spending significant time with your designer and every engineer, they can't see the enthusiasm in your eyes. If not co-located, travel to them at least every couple months. This pays off big in motivation and velocity.

> At midsize-to-large companies, product marketing typically evangelizes to customers and the sales force. The PM still helps on big deals/partnerships but focuses evangelism on the *team* — the best thing you can do for customers is give them a great product.

## Managing Stakeholders

**Who is a stakeholder?** The practical test: someone with **veto power** or who can otherwise prevent your work from launching. Typically: the executive team (CEO + leaders of marketing, sales, technology), business partners, finance, legal, compliance, and business development. Everyone else is part of the broader community — a source of input, not a stakeholder. Startups have few stakeholders (little to lose); large companies have many (protecting substantial assets).

**The PM's responsibility:** Understand each stakeholder's considerations and constraints and bring that knowledge into the team. Building something customers love but that legal/security/finance won't let you deploy destroys the company's confidence in the product team. Beyond understanding constraints, the PM must *sincerely* convince each stakeholder that she will solve for their concerns too — otherwise they'll escalate or try to control.

**What success looks like:** Stakeholders respect you, trust you understand their concerns, trust you'll keep them informed, and give you room to find the best solutions — even ones very different from what they first envisioned. The foundation is competence: deep understanding of customers, analytics, technology, industry, and especially the business. The main way you demonstrate this is by sharing openly what you learn.

Key techniques:

- **Spend 1:1 time and listen.** Sit down with each key stakeholder, listen, ask lots of questions, be transparent. Explain that the better you understand their constraints, the better your solutions. Budget ~2–3 hours/week — roughly half an hour with each key stakeholder. A weekly lunch or coffee with your most-involved stakeholders works well.
- **Preview solutions during discovery, BEFORE you build.** A common career-limiting mistake is showing stakeholders the solution *after* it's built. Commit to previewing solutions during discovery — before the work hits the backlog. Discovery isn't only about validating value/usability (customers) and feasibility (engineers); it's also about ensuring stakeholders will support the solution.
- **Move disputes from opinions to data.** When it's the PM's opinion vs. the stakeholder's, the stakeholder usually wins (more senior). Change the game: quickly run a test, collect evidence, share openly. Maybe neither opinion was right. Discovery is built for exactly these tests.
- **No design-by-committee, no presentation sign-offs.** Don't gather all stakeholders in a big meeting with a PowerPoint to test business viability. Two career-limiting problems:
  1. **Presentations are terrible for testing viability** — too ambiguous. A lawyer needs the actual screens/wording; marketing wants the actual design; security needs to see exactly what the product does. **Only trust a sign-off on a high-fidelity prototype.** Execs who approved a slide deck are often shocked and angry when they see the real product.
  2. **A group setting yields design by committee** — mediocre results. Instead, meet *privately* with each stakeholder, show the high-fidelity prototype, and let them raise concerns one at a time.

This sounds like more work, but in the long run it's far less work, time, and grief. Note: some stakeholders may not understand the product role and may feel threatened by it — be sensitive and be ready to explain how technology-enabled product companies operate.

## Communicating Product Learnings

In a startup this happens naturally (the team *is* the company). At scale it's much harder but more important.

**The technique:** At a company all-hands (or similar) **every week or two**, the **head of product / VP product** takes **15–30 minutes** to highlight what's been learned in product discovery across the teams — what worked, what didn't, and what teams plan to try next.

Rules:
- Keep it at the **big-learnings level**, moving fast. This is *not* every PM parading through a 1–2 hour detailed update, and *not* a repeat of sprint reviews. That's why the VP does it.

Why it works (tactical + cultural):
- Big learnings spread broadly, especially when results disappoint — and sometimes someone in the audience has the insight that explains the result.
- Teams stay apprised of what others are learning; learnings reach leaders.
- It pushes teams to focus on big learnings, not minor experiments with no real impact.
- It reinforces that discovery/innovation is continuous rapid experimentation and learning.
- It signals that the product org is transparent and generous, and that it exists not "to serve the business" but to **solve customer problems in ways that work for the business**.

## Quick workflow

1. **Evangelize** — build a prototype, share pain + vision + learnings, master the demo, show genuine enthusiasm, spend face time. Aim for missionaries.
2. **Align stakeholders** — identify who has veto power; spend 1:1 time listening; preview high-fidelity prototypes during discovery before building; move disputes to data; never sign off on a presentation; never design by committee.
3. **Communicate learnings** — VP-led 15–30 min all-hands every 1–2 weeks at the big-learnings level.
4. **Transform** — see `transformation-techniques.md` (discovery sprint, pilot team, weaning off roadmaps).
5. **Diagnose & establish culture** — see `culture-diagnostics.md` (good/bad team, loss of innovation/velocity, innovation/execution dimensions).
