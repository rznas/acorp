# PM Competency Self-Assessment & Ramp Plan

Use this to assess where you stand on the four key competencies and to structure your first two to three months in a product manager role. Score each item honestly: **0 = not yet, 1 = partial, 2 = solid, go-to person**.

## 1. Deep Knowledge of the Customer

- [ ] I can name our top customer segments and their primary pains, desires, and how they think.
- [ ] (B2B) I understand how customers actually *work* and how they *decide to buy*.
- [ ] I do regular qualitative learning (interviews, observation) to understand *why* customers behave as they do.
- [ ] I am the team's acknowledged go-to expert on the customer.
- [ ] I am an undisputed expert on our actual product (every feature, every flow).

## 2. Deep Knowledge of the Data

- [ ] I start most days with ~30 min in analytics, reviewing the last 24 hours.
- [ ] I review sales analytics, usage analytics, and A/B test results without needing them interpreted for me.
- [ ] I can pull and read the data myself (trained on the tools); I don't fully delegate understanding to an analyst.
- [ ] I combine quantitative (what) with qualitative (why) to form a complete picture.

## 3. Deep Knowledge of Your Business & Stakeholders

- [ ] I can list every key stakeholder: general management, sales, marketing, finance, legal, business development, customer service, CEO.
- [ ] For each stakeholder, I know the **constraints** they operate under.
- [ ] Each key stakeholder believes I understand their constraints.
- [ ] Each key stakeholder trusts I will only bring solutions consistent with those constraints.
- [ ] I understand our business model and key KPIs (LTV, ARPU, customer acquisition cost, cost of sales, contribution margin).
- [ ] I can articulate the role my product plays in the overall business.

## 4. Deep Knowledge of Your Market & Industry

- [ ] I can name our main competitors and where we are substantially better (parity is not enough).
- [ ] I track relevant technology trends (including AI/ML where applicable).
- [ ] I follow customer-behavior shifts, relevant industry analysts, and social media for our market.
- [ ] I understand the ecosystem our product fits into and how we add value to it.
- [ ] I am building for where the market will be tomorrow, not where it was yesterday.

## Character Check (Smart, Creative, Persistent)

- [ ] **Smart:** I'm intellectually curious and quickly learn/apply new technologies to customer problems.
- [ ] **Creative:** I think beyond features to solve business problems.
- [ ] **Persistent:** I push past resistance with evidence, constant communication, and cross-functional bridge-building.
- [ ] **Passion:** I genuinely care about the product and customers (the work follows me home, and that's okay).

## PM-Engineer Relationship Check

- [ ] I have working technology literacy (took a real programming course or equivalent).
- [ ] I never bluff with engineers; I say "I'll find out" when I don't know.
- [ ] I share customer pain, data, and business constraints openly with engineers.
- [ ] I bring problems, not prescribed solutions — engineers have latitude on *how*.
- [ ] I engage engineers every workday (discovery input + delivery clarifications).
- [ ] I work closely with the tech lead on discovery.
- [ ] My engineers feel like missionaries, not mercenaries.

## Foundational Courses

- [ ] Introduction to Computer Programming (a graded course, any real language — not HTML).
- [ ] Introduction to Business Accounting/Finance (or a strong general marketing course covering KPIs).

## Two-to-Three-Month Ramp Plan

This assumes a manager who gives you access to customers, data (and tool training), stakeholders, and time. Adjust pacing to your context.

### Weeks 1-3 — Customer & Product
- Meet/observe real users and customers; document their pains and how they decide to buy.
- Learn every part of the product; become the in-house product expert.
- Set up and learn the analytics tools; begin a daily analytics habit.
- Start sharing what you learn openly — good and bad.

### Weeks 4-6 — Data & Business
- Build fluency in the dashboards: sales, usage, A/B tests.
- Map every key stakeholder and schedule 1:1s.
- For each stakeholder, capture their constraints in writing.
- Learn the business model and the KPIs that matter.

### Weeks 7-9 — Stakeholders & Market
- Confirm with each stakeholder that you understand their constraints and will respect them.
- Map competitors and the ecosystem; identify where you must be substantially better.
- Identify the technology trends shaping your market.

### Weeks 8-12 — Team & First Wins
- Deepen the collaborative relationship with your product team; engage engineers daily.
- Work closely with the tech lead on early discovery.
- Take a first opportunity through discovery → spec → delivery, modeling Jane Manning's approach: understand each objector's constraints, then advocate a solution that satisfies them while serving customers and the business.

### Ongoing Cadence
- Daily: ~30 min analytics; engage engineers (discovery + delivery).
- Weekly: customer contact; stakeholder touchpoints as needed.
- Continuous: track market/industry/competitor and technology trends.
