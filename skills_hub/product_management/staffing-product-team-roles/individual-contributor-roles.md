# Individual-Contributor and Supporting Roles

## Product Designer

A full discovery partner who sits side by side with the PM, not a downstream service. Design *informs* functionality at least as much as functionality drives design. Measured on the **success of the product**, not the output of their design work — so designers share the PM's concerns: real customers, customer value, and business constraints.

Modern product designers are responsible for:

- **Product discovery** — continuous collaboration with PM and engineers, from discovery to delivery. They share the PM's orientation toward customers, value, and business viability.
- **Holistic user experience design** — UX is much bigger than UI ("customer experience"). It covers *every* touch point over time: how customers learn about the product, onboarding, motivation to deeper commitment, moments of gratification, sharing, offline services (e.g., the Uber ride, the Airbnb stay), perceived responsiveness. They design the customer journey across time, not just screens.
- **Prototyping** — prototypes are their primary canvas for communicating ideas internally and externally; fluent across multiple prototyping tools and fidelities.
- **User testing** — built into a weekly cadence (not just when a prototype is "ready"), to validate, refine, and gather unsought insights, and to avoid getting attached to ideas. Tests *value* (will customers use/buy it?), which is broader than usability.
- **Interaction and visual design** — interaction design = conceptual models, task flows, control layouts; visual design = composition, typography, brand expression. Modern designers carry some skill in both (critical on mobile, where the two are intertwined). For hardware, **industrial design** (materials, design for manufacturing) is also needed.

**Common design failures (the absence of product design):**
1. The PM (untrained in design) does the design — usually hands engineers wireframes and the engineers cobble together the visuals.
2. The PM gives engineers only high-level user stories, so engineers invent the design themselves.
3. The PM provides interaction design/wireframes and a separate graphic designer does the visuals.

All three fail to produce a holistic design. A related organizational failure: bringing design in-house but running it as an **internal agency** (separate studio, request-and-receive) — if that were how it should work, you'd just keep using external agencies.

**Why it matters:** Strong design is table stakes for consumer products and a top competitive differentiator for B2B (and table stakes for small-business products, where the user is the buyer).

**Five keys to a healthy PM–designer relationship:**
1. Sit your designer next to you.
2. Include the designer from the inception of every idea.
3. Bring the designer to as many customer/user interactions as possible — learn together.
4. Resist giving the designer your own design ideas; give room to solve the problem.
5. Encourage early, frequent iteration — don't nitpick early iterations; encourage exploring alternative solutions, not just refining one approach.

**If you have no designer:** the PM ends up doing it (a failure mode above). The user researcher's duties also fall to the designer when there's no researcher.

## Product Marketing Manager (PMM)

Different from other team members because the PMM is usually **not a full-time, dedicated** member of any single team. Product marketing is organized by customer-facing product, target market, or go-to-market channel (e.g., enterprise, vertical, mid-market), and there are fewer PMMs than teams, so they **spread across multiple product teams**.

**Owns:** representing the *market* to the product team — **positioning** (the market position the product must occupy), **messaging** (digital/content assets, sales tools, training that enable sales), and a winning **go-to-market plan**. Deeply engaged with the sales channel; knows its capabilities, limitations, and current competitive issues.

**Why it matters:** "Works for our business" largely means there's a real (large enough) market, you can differentiate from competitors, you can cost-effectively acquire/engage customers, and you have the GTM channels to deliver the product. The PMM is the critical partner on all of this, engaged throughout discovery, delivery, and GTM — e.g., ensuring the team gets signal from a broad enough slice of the market, and shaping messaging and the GTM plan from early product signals.

**Failure modes:**
- No PMM in a company with a sales force → enabling sales falls on the PM and can become a full-time job; then no one is figuring out the product to sell.
- Direct-to-consumer companies → marketing chases clicks and brand at the expense of a differentiated market position.
- Reverting to the **old model** where product marketing *defines* the product and PM only delivers it — that is explicitly not the modern role.

A strong PMM does not diminish the PM's responsibility for a successful product; the best relationships understand their distinct roles and that they are essential to each other's success.

## Supporting Roles

Typically shared across a small number of teams, not dedicated. At a small startup you may have none of these and must cover the activities yourself.

### User Researchers
Trained in qualitative techniques (some also quantitative). Product discovery uses two kinds of rapid learning: **qualitative** and **quantitative**. Within qualitative, research is either **generative** (understanding which problems to solve) or **evaluative** (assessing how well solutions solve the problem). Researchers help find the right users, craft the right tests, and learn the most from each interaction.

**Key:** learning must be **shared learning** — the PM must witness insights first-hand. Do *not* delegate the learning and just receive a report.

**If absent:** the product designer picks up these responsibilities.

### Data Analysts (BI Analysts)
Support quantitative learning: collect the right analytics, manage data-privacy constraints, analyze data, plan live-data tests, and interpret results. Experts in the data the business collects and reports — "gold mines" given how data-driven product work is. In data-heavy companies (e.g., large consumer companies) this can be a dedicated, co-located role.

**If absent:** falls on the PM, who must spend significant time diving deep into the data to make good decisions.

### Test Automation Engineers
Write automated tests; have largely replaced manual QA. Models vary by engineering leadership: engineers may write all tests, or (commonly) a blend — engineers write unit-level tests, test engineers write higher-level automated tests. Complex products may have multiple test engineers per team.

**Not okay:** no test engineers, engineers don't test, and the company expects the **PM to do quality testing**. PM acceptance testing ("generally as expected before go-live") is a far cry from the significant automation needed to **release with confidence**.

### Delivery Managers
A special type of project manager whose mission is **removing obstacles (impediments)** for the team — chasing decisions/approvals in other functions, coordinating cross-team dependencies, persuading a designer to produce assets, etc. Typically also the team's **Scrum Master**. They speed delivery by removing roadblocks, *not* by cracking the whip.

May carry the title project manager or program manager — but only if defined as obstacle-removal, **not** old-style program management.

**If absent:** project-management work falls to the PM and engineering managers. Fine for small orgs (even advantageous), but increasingly important at ~5–10+ teams, where PMs otherwise lose time for their primary job: ensuring engineers have a product worth building.
