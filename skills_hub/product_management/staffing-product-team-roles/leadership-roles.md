# Leadership Roles

## The Role of Leadership and the Holistic View of Product

The primary job of leadership in any tech org is to **recruit, develop, and retain strong talent**. In a product company it goes further: maintaining a **holistic view of product** — connecting the dots across teams so the whole product hangs together. Easy with 1–2 teams; hard as the company grows. Three holistic-view leaders cover three dimensions:

1. **Leaders of Product Management** — ensure the whole system fits together from a *business* point of view (vision, strategy, functionality, business rules/logic). Done by the head of product/directors, or by a **principal product manager** (a very senior IC, ~director-level, reporting to the head of product). Because the head of product is first responsible for building PMs' skills, a dedicated principal PM can focus on the *product* itself and be a resource to all PMs, designers, engineers, and test staff. Critical for large/complex/legacy systems.
2. **Leaders of Product Design** — ensure a consistent, effective UX system-wide. Head of design, a design director, or a principal designer. Someone must review everything user-visible because no single PM/designer can hold the whole customer journey in their head.
3. **Leaders of Technology** — ensure the system fits together from a *technology* point of view. The CTO/VP engineering, helped by engineering managers, directors, and architects: review architecture and systems design of all software (in-house and vendor), and own a clear strategy for managing **technical debt**.

**Absence symptoms:** inconsistent agency-like UX → missing head of design/principal designer; PMs who don't grasp the implications of decisions and keep asking devs how the system works → missing principal PM; spaghetti software where simple changes take forever → technical debt.

**Protecting these people:** they are rare and valuable; don't lose them, don't force them into management just to earn more, and have each develop a strong second. Documentation does **not** substitute — systems outgrow docs, and the definitive (current) answer lives in the source code. Best practice: seat the head of product, head of design, and head of technology very close together.

## Head of Product (VP Product / CPO)

The most senior product role in the company or business unit. Typically manages PMs and designers (sometimes data analysts), reports to the CEO, and should be a **peer** to the CTO and VP marketing. A hard role to do well; great product leaders are highly valued and often go on to found companies.

**Four key competencies:**

1. **Team development** — *the single most important responsibility:* develop a strong team of PMs and designers via recruiting, training, and ongoing coaching. Developing great people is a different skill than developing great products. Hire someone with a *proven* track record of developing others. Failure mode: promoting a weak performer into the role because "people like him" — they can't develop others into strong performers, and it signals the wrong thing.
2. **Product vision and strategy** — vision drives and sustains the company. The VP must **complement** the CEO. Two bad situations: (a) a strong-visionary CEO hires a VP in her own image → clash and short tenure (revolving-door symptom); (b) a non-visionary CEO hires in her own image → no clash but a vision void, poor morale, no innovation. Risk: a visionary founder leaves, leaving only an execution-strong product leader and no vision source (prefer founders stay on). If the CEO wrongly thinks she's a visionary, you need a head of product who is visionary yet able to convince the CEO it was her idea.
3. **Execution** — vision means nothing if you can't get product into customers' hands. Must be an expert in modern product planning, customer discovery, product discovery, and development process, and able to operate at your org's scale. Bigger org → more critical are **stakeholder management** and **internal evangelism** to align everyone.
4. **Product culture** — what makes a good org *great*: continuous rapid testing and learning, making mistakes quickly while mitigating risk, continuous innovation, true collaboration, respecting designers and engineers, and the power of a motivated team. The VP should give real examples and concrete plans for instilling it.

Plus **experience** (strong technology background + understanding of your business/market economics and dynamics; domain experience as needed) and **chemistry** (must work well personally with the CEO, CTO, and heads of marketing/design — interview with a long, personal dinner).

### Group Product Manager (GPM)
A hybrid **player-coach** role: a proven (usually senior) PM who is the actual PM for one team **and** develops/coaches 1–3 other PMs. Designed for **tightly-coupled** team groups (e.g., a marketplace split into buyer-side, seller-side, platform groups, each led by a GPM) so a multi-team experience stays seamless. A great way to taste both career paths before committing.

### Two PM career paths
- **Individual contributor → Principal Product Manager:** a rock-star IC who tackles the toughest product work; highly regarded, compensated like a director or VP.
- **Management → Director of Product Management:** 3–10 PMs report in; responsible for (1) ensuring those PMs are strong and (2) product vision/strategy and connecting the dots across teams (holistic view). GPMs may go on to director/VP, to principal PM, or stay as GPM.

## Head of Technology (CTO / VP Engineering)

Leads the org responsible for **architecture, engineering, quality, site operations, site security, release management, and usually delivery management** — building and running the company's products and services. Warning flag: if technology reports to a **CIO** (a very different role), expect the pathologies of failed product efforts. The hallmark of a great CTO is treating technology as a **strategic enabler** — removing technology as a barrier and broadening "the art of the possible" for business and product leaders.

**Six responsibilities (priority order):**

1. **Organization** — build an excellent org with a strong management team committed to developing employees. *Measured by:* development plans for all employees, retention rate, and how the rest of the company evaluates the managers and the org.
2. **Leadership** — represent technology in the company's strategic direction; inform direction, M&A, and build/buy/partner decisions.
3. **Delivery** — rapidly, reliably, repeatedly deliver quality product. *Measured by:* consistency/frequency of release vehicles and quality/reliability of launched software. The main obstacle is **technical debt**; the CTO must keep it manageable.
4. **Architecture** — ensure architecture delivers needed functionality, scalability, reliability, security, and performance; orchestrate a cohesive company-wide technology strategy (the sum, not just the parts). *Measured by:* continuous monitoring/advancement of infrastructure and customer-impacting outages due to infrastructure/architecture.
5. **Discovery** — ensure senior engineers/architects participate actively in product discovery. If engineers are used only to write software, you get a fraction of their value. *Measured by:* engineering participation in discovery (duration and coverage) and frequency of innovations credited to engineers.
6. **Evangelism** — serve as the company spokesperson for engineering; community leadership with developers, partners, and customers. *Measured by:* university-relations/recruitment programs and sponsoring/participating in developer-community events.

A good PM–CTO working relationship makes the job great; a bad one makes for very tough days. Build it (e.g., regular lunches to surface each other's biggest challenges).
