# Setting Product Objectives with OKRs

Replace feature roadmaps with empowered, outcome-based objectives. Tell teams the
business problem to solve and how success is measured; let them figure out the
solution. This skill covers why roadmaps fail, the alternative, and the OKR
technique end-to-end including scale.

## When to use this skill

- Designing how a product org decides what teams work on
- Writing, reviewing, or scoring OKRs for product teams
- Pushing back on a feature roadmap or a stakeholder demanding committed dates
- Setting OKR cadence, tracking, scoring, and accountability
- Scaling objectives across 20-50 teams, platform teams, and business units

## Why product roadmaps fail: two inconvenient truths

1. **At least half of our product ideas just won't work.** An idea can fail on
   any of four risks:
   - *Value* — customers aren't excited enough to use/buy it (most common)
   - *Usability* — they want it but it's too hard to use
   - *Feasibility* — it's far more costly/involved to build than expected
   - *Business viability* — legal, financial, or business constraints block it
2. **Even the ideas that do work take several iterations** to reach the expected
   business value (the *time to money* problem).

The list of ideas isn't itself the problem. The problem is that anything on a
document titled "roadmap" — regardless of disclaimers — is read across the
company as a **commitment**. The team is then on the hook to ship the feature
even when it doesn't solve the underlying problem.

Weak teams plod through the assigned roadmap and blame stakeholders when items
fail. Strong teams embrace these truths and use **product discovery** to tackle
the four risks fast (hours and days, not weeks and months) before committing.

## The alternative to roadmaps

Roadmaps survive because they serve two real needs that don't go away. Any
alternative must meet both at least as well:
- Management wants teams working on the **highest-business-value items first**.
- The business sometimes needs **date-based commitments** it can track.

Empowered teams can solve assigned problems themselves only if they have
**business context**, which has two components:

1. **Product vision and strategy** — the big-picture of what the org is trying to
   accomplish and the plan to get there. Each team's focus rolls up to the vision.
2. **Business objectives** — the specific, prioritized problems for each team.

The idea: tell the team what to accomplish and how results are measured, then let
them find the best solution. Management's job is to **prioritize business
results, not product ideas**. This addresses the first roadmap driver. The
second driver (hard dates) is handled by *high-integrity commitments* (below).

It is all about **outcome rather than output**:
- Teams are more motivated solving problems their own way (missionaries, not mercenaries).
- A team isn't done just by shipping a requested feature — the feature must
  *solve the business problem* (per the key results), or the team tries again.
- The first approach often fails no matter who proposed it; this model expects that.

**Outcome-based roadmaps** (each item stated as a business problem to solve, not a
feature) are essentially equivalent to an OKR-based system — the format differs
more than the content. Watch out: teams often slap a deadline on *every* item
rather than only the ones with a true date constraint, which hurts culture and
motivation.

### High-integrity commitments

The root cause of commitment grief is *when* commitments are made: too early —
before we know if we can deliver it and whether it solves the customer's problem.

The fix is give-and-take:
1. The team asks executives/stakeholders for a little time in **product discovery**
   to validate value, usability (with customers), feasibility (with engineers),
   and viability (with stakeholders).
2. After discovery, once there's a solution that works for the business, the team
   makes an **informed, high-integrity commitment** on date and expected results.

Delivery managers are key — they track commitments and dependencies (engineering
estimates mean nothing if the team can't start for a month). Good companies
**minimize** these commitments, but when they make one, the company can depend on it.

## The OKR technique

OKR is a tool for management, focus, and alignment. Critical points for product orgs:

1. **Objectives are qualitative; key results are quantitative/measurable.**
2. **Key results measure *business results*, not output or tasks.**
3. Focus on the **organization's objectives** and each **product team's
   objectives** (which roll up to the org's). Don't let personal or functional-team
   objectives dilute the focus.
4. Pick a **cadence**: typically annual for org objectives, quarterly for team objectives.
5. Keep counts **small**: 1-3 objectives, each with 1-3 key results.
6. Every team **tracks active progress** against objectives, typically weekly.
7. Objectives needn't cover every little thing — just what the team needs to accomplish.
8. Teams must feel **accountable**. On substantial failure, run a post-mortem with peers/management.
9. Agree org-wide on a consistent **scoring** approach so teams can depend on each other.
10. Mark clearly when a key result is actually a **high-integrity commitment** — those
    are binary (delivered or not), unlike normal KRs where you aim for ~0.7.
11. Be **transparent** across product/tech on each team's objectives and progress.
12. **Ownership:** Senior management (CEO/exec team) owns org OKRs. Heads of
    product/technology own product-team objectives. Individual teams propose the
    key results for objectives they're assigned. Expect quarterly give-and-take.

### Scoring scale (0 to 1.0)

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 0     | Essentially no progress |
| 0.3   | Bare minimum — what you knew you could achieve |
| 0.7   | More than the minimum; really did what you'd hoped (target for normal KRs) |
| 1.0   | Truly exceptional, beyond what anyone hoped |

### Worked example

> **Situation:** New customer onboarding currently takes 30 days. To scale, it
> must drop to three hours or less.
>
> - **Objective (qualitative):** Dramatically reduce the time it takes for a new
>   customer to go live.
> - **Key Result (quantitative, business result):** Average new-customer
>   onboarding time less than three hours.

Note the KR measures the business outcome (onboarding time), not output (e.g.,
"ship the new onboarding wizard").

## Deploy OKRs at the product-team level, not the functional level

A product team is **cross-functional** — typically a product manager, a product
designer, and a few engineers (sometimes a data analyst, user researcher, or test
automation engineer). Larger orgs run 20-50 such teams, each owning a significant
area (e.g., driver app, rider app, payments).

A common mistake when starting OKRs: each **functional department** writes its own
OKRs — design pursues responsive design, engineering pursues re-platforming, QA
pursues test automation. But the members of those departments are the actual
members of cross-functional product teams, which have *business* objectives
(reduce CAC, increase DAU, cut onboarding time). Individuals then get conflicting
direction cascaded from functional managers, producing confusion, frustration,
and poor results.

**The key: focus OKRs at the *product team* level.** Cascading runs **up** from
cross-functional teams to the company/business-unit level — not down through
functions.

Acceptable exceptions:
- **Functional managers** (head of UX, engineering, QA, product) may have their own
  org-level objectives (responsive-design strategy, technical-debt strategy, tool
  selection, product vision) — they don't serve on a product team, so they aren't conflicted.
- **Individual contributors** may have a small number of personal growth objectives,
  as long as they don't interfere with their primary duty to their product team.
- Large functional initiatives (responsive design, tech debt, test automation)
  should be prioritized at the leadership level alongside business objectives and
  then **incorporated into the relevant product team's objectives**.

## OKRs at scale (growth-stage / enterprise)

A tool to manage and align work is essential to scaling, but scale shifts more
burden onto leadership and management:

1. **Clear org-level objectives.** With ~25 teams and, say, two org objectives
   (improve customer lifetime value; expand globally), leadership (heads of
   product, technology, design) must decide which teams pursue which objective —
   some one, some both, some critical work beyond the two.
2. **Platform / shared-services teams.** Many teams exist to support other teams.
   They're high-leverage but serve customers *indirectly*. Leadership coordinates
   their objectives, dependencies, and aligned interests.
3. **Reconciliation.** Leadership reviews proposed key results across teams,
   finds **gaps**, and adjusts (enlist more teams, re-prioritize work).
4. **Transparency at scale.** It's harder to know who's doing what; online tools
   help, but management still connects the dots between teams.
5. **Managing commitments and dependencies.** The larger the org, the longer the
   list of high-integrity commitments and the more actively they must be tracked —
   delivery managers play the key role.
6. **Corporate and business-unit OKRs.** Multi-BU enterprises have corporate-level
   *and* business-unit-level OKRs; product teams roll up into those.

Bottom line at scale: leadership must ensure the org is truly aligned and that
every team understands how it fits and what it's there to contribute.
