# Crafting Product Vision and Strategy

Empowered product teams can only act with autonomy when they understand the broader context. That context starts with a clear, compelling **product vision** and the **product strategy** that is the path to achieving it. Add **product principles** to describe the nature of the products you build.

> Most important: the product vision should be **inspiring**, and the product strategy should be **focused**.

Use this skill when defining, reviewing, or critiquing any of these three artifacts, or when deciding which market to tackle next.

## Core distinction: vision vs strategy

| | Product Vision | Product Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Describes | The future you want to create | The path to get there |
| Horizon | 2-5 years (5-10 for hardware/devices) | A sequence of nearer-term releases |
| Form | Storyboard, narrative/white paper, or **visiontype** prototype | A prioritized sequence of product/market fits |
| Job | **Inspire** (leadership) | **Focus** / get us there (management) |
| Quality goal | Inspiring | Focused |

The analogy: vision is to strategy as good leadership is to good management. Leadership *inspires* and sets direction; management helps *get us there*.

Vision is **not** the company mission statement (e.g., "organize the world's information"). Mission says *what*; vision says *how we plan to accomplish it*. Vision is **not** a spec — it is a persuasive piece. Its primary purpose is to communicate the vision and inspire teams, stakeholders, investors, partners, and prospective customers to help make it real. Done well, it is one of your most effective **recruiting** tools and motivates strong technologists, who are drawn to meaningful, inspiring work.

You can test a vision somewhat, but buying into a vision is always a **leap of faith** — you likely don't know how, or even if, you'll deliver it. You have several years to discover the solutions; at this stage you only need to believe it's a worthwhile pursuit.

### Scope: one vision per organization
The idea is NOT that every product team has its own vision — that misses the point. The *organization* has one product vision, and all teams contribute to making it real. In very large companies, the mission may apply to the whole company while each business unit has its own vision and strategy.

## Building the product strategy

Trying to please everybody at once will please nobody. So do **not** embark on a giant multi-year effort to deliver the whole vision in one release.

The product strategy is the **sequence of products or releases** you plan to deliver on the path to the vision ("products or releases" loosely: versions of one product, a series of related products, or meaningful milestones).

For most businesses, construct the strategy around a **series of product/market fits**. Common ways to sequence them:

- **By vertical market** (business-focused): financial services, then manufacturing, then automotive.
- **By customer/user persona** (consumer-focused): high-school students, then college students, then working learners.
- **By geography**: tackle regions of the world in an intentional sequence.
- **By milestone**: e.g., first ship ratings/reviews to developers; next, use that data to build a consumer-sentiment database; then leverage it for advanced product recommendations.

There is no single ideal approach, and you can never know how a different sequence would have gone. **The most important benefit is simply deciding to focus on a single target market at a time.** All teams know which market is current (e.g., "we're tackling manufacturing now"), and the goal is the **smallest actual deliverable product that makes those customers successful**. Ideas pertaining to other markets are saved for future consideration.

Beyond raising the odds of shipping something that powers the business, the strategy is your tool to **align with sales and marketing**: sell only in markets where you've demonstrated product/market fit. As soon as you prove fit in a new market (typically via an initial set of **reference customers**), point the sales force at finding as many more customers in that market as possible.

## Prioritizing markets: TAM / GTM / TTM

Prioritize markets and focus on one at a time. There's no single right way, but three critical inputs dominate:

1. **TAM — Total Addressable Market (sizing).** All else equal, prefer big markets. But all else is rarely equal: if the largest market needs two years of work while a smaller-but-still-significant one is much closer to market, almost everyone (CEO, head of sales) prefers delivering the smaller one sooner.
2. **GTM — Go To Market (distribution).** Different markets may need different sales channels. Even a larger market may lose to a smaller one that **leverages your existing sales channels** rather than requiring a new one.
3. **TTM — Time To Market.** A (very rough) estimate of how long each will take.

Other factors can matter too. Typically the **head of product, head of technology, and head of product marketing** sit down together to work out the strategy, balancing these factors.

## The 10 principles of product vision

1. **Start with *why*.** (After Simon Sinek.) Use the vision to articulate your *purpose*. Everything follows from that.
2. **Fall in love with the problem, not the solution.** Many product people struggle with this; it's essential.
3. **Don't be afraid to think big.** Too many visions aren't ambitious enough — achievable in 6-12 months, not substantial enough to inspire anyone.
4. **Don't be afraid to disrupt yourselves** — because if you don't, someone else will. Don't pour effort into protecting what you have instead of creating new customer value.
5. **The vision needs to inspire.** You need teams of **missionaries, not mercenaries**. Vision drives missionary-like passion. Make it meaningful by focusing on how you genuinely help users and customers.
6. **Determine and embrace relevant, meaningful trends.** Identifying trends isn't hard; the hard part is helping the org see how those trends let your products solve customer problems in new and better ways.
7. **Skate to where the puck is heading, not where it was.** Identify what is changing — and what won't change — within the vision's time frame. Avoid being either wildly optimistic or far too conservative. This is usually the hardest part of a good vision.
8. **Be stubborn on vision but flexible on details** (Jeff Bezos). Many teams abandon their vision far too soon — a **vision pivot**, usually a sign of a weak product org. But don't get attached to details; adjusting course to reach the destination is a **discovery pivot**, and that's fine. (See pivots below.)
9. **Any product vision is a leap of faith.** If you can truly validate it, it isn't ambitious enough. It will take years to know. Make sure it's meaningful and recruit people who are passionate about the problem and willing to work several years to realize it.
10. **Evangelize continuously and relentlessly.** There's no over-communicating a vision, especially in large orgs. People in all corners will get nervous about something they see or hear — reassure them quickly before their fear infects others.

### Vision pivot vs discovery pivot
- **Vision pivot** = giving up on / changing the vision itself. Usually premature and a sign of organizational weakness. Be stubborn here.
- **Discovery pivot** = adjusting the details/course while keeping the vision. Expected and healthy. Be flexible here.

## The 5 principles of product strategy

Good strategies share these five:

1. **Focus on one target market or persona at a time.** Don't try to please everyone in one release. The product will still be useful to others, but it will be *loved* by some — and that's key.
2. **Align with the business strategy.** The org exists to deliver on the business strategy. If that involves a new monetization model, the product strategy must align with it.
3. **Align with the sales and go-to-market strategy.** A new sales/marketing channel can have far-reaching impact on the product; keep the strategy aligned with it.
4. **Obsess over customers, not competitors.** When a serious competitor appears, many companies panic and chase the competitor instead of serving customers. Don't ignore the market, but remember: **customers rarely leave for a competitor — they leave because you stopped taking care of them.**
5. **Communicate the strategy across the organization.** Part of evangelizing the vision: everyone should know which customers are the focus now and which are planned for later. Stay especially synced with **sales, marketing, finance, and service**.

## Product principles

Complement vision and strategy with a set of **product principles** that speak to the **nature of the products you want to create**.

- Principles are **not** a feature list and are **not** tied to any one release. They align with the vision for an entire product line.
- They reflect **what the company and product teams believe is important**, and serve as a **conflict-resolution guide** for the countless decisions teams face.
- **Example (eBay):** Most revenue came from sellers, so there was strong incentive to please sellers — but sellers loved eBay because it provided **buyers**. This produced the principle: *"In cases where the needs of buyers and sellers conflict, we will prioritize the needs of the buyer, because that's actually the most important thing we can do for sellers."* A single principle like this resolves a huge number of marketplace design decisions.
- Whether to publish principles depends on purpose: often they're an internal tool for product teams; sometimes they're a public statement of belief for users, customers, partners, suppliers, investors, and employees.

## How to apply this skill

1. **Confirm the artifact.** Vision (the inspiring multi-year future), strategy (the focused sequence of fits), or principles (the nature of the products) — they are distinct; don't conflate them.
2. **Vision check:** Is there ONE org-level vision? Is it inspiring, ambitious, purpose-driven (*why*), problem-focused, trend-aware, and forward-looking? Is it captured as a storyboard, narrative, or visiontype — not a spec? Does it serve to recruit and motivate?
3. **Strategy check:** Is it a sequence of product/market fits focused on one target market/persona at a time? Is it aligned with business and GTM strategy? Are markets prioritized with TAM/GTM/TTM (worked out by product + tech + product marketing)? Does each step aim for the smallest deliverable that makes that market's customers successful, validated by reference customers?
4. **Principles check:** Do they describe the products' nature and act as a conflict-resolution guide (like eBay's buyer-over-seller)?
5. **Distinguish pivots:** Resist vision pivots (be stubborn); embrace discovery pivots (be flexible).
6. **Evangelize:** Communicate vision and strategy relentlessly and keep sales, marketing, finance, and service in sync.

These artifacts are the context that feeds team objectives (OKRs) and lets empowered teams choose well.
