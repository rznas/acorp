# Customer Discovery Program — Detailed Reference

From INSPIRED (Marty Cagan), Chapter 39. The technique develops a set of **reference customers in parallel** with discovering and developing the actual product. It takes substantial effort (mostly the product manager's) but is the single best leading indicator of future product success.

## The power of reference customers

A **reference customer** is:
- a **real** customer (not friends or family),
- running your product in **production** (not a trial or prototype),
- who has paid **real money** (not given the product free), and
- who is **willing to tell others** how much they love it (voluntarily and sincerely).

Why it matters: reference customers are the single best sales/marketing tool you can provide, and they change the dynamic between product and the rest of the company. Without them, sales can't find real product/market fit, sells whatever and however they can, and that's the product org's fault — not theirs.

## When to run it

Larger efforts only: a new product or business, taking an existing product to a new market/geography, or a redesign. **Not** for features or minor projects.

The basic driver: for a significant new product, the most common objection is "show me other companies like us already succeeding with this." More references are better; too few (one or two) makes prospects fear the product only works for those special cases.

## Single target market

Develop **six** reference customers in **one specific target market or segment** — six similar customers. Six is about confidence, not statistical significance, and the number has held up over time.

Do **not** spread across two or three markets — you lose the focus this program provides. Pursue the vision one vertical (or one geography) at a time: e.g., six references for financial services, then six for manufacturing; or six for the US, then Germany, then Brazil.

Do your best **not to launch in the market until you have the six references** — don't turn on sales/marketing until you have evidence you can make customers successful.

## Recruiting prospective reference customers

Recruit **6–8** (to still end with six if one or two aren't a match or become unavailable). From existing customers, prospects, or a blend. Criteria:

- **Truly feel the pain** — near desperate for the solution; if a solution existed elsewhere they'd have bought it.
- **Screen out technologists** — people drawn by the technology rather than the business value.
- **Have people and time** to work closely with you — test early prototypes, give detailed feedback, run early versions in their environment.
- **Marquee names** if possible — most valuable to sales and marketing.

The PM does this in tight collaboration with the **product marketing manager**.

Reality check / demand validation: if you can't even recruit four or five interested prospects, you may be chasing a problem that isn't important enough — and will struggle to sell the product. Rethink your plans. (Every market has a hungry, desperate segment; people who say "come back once you have references" are useful leads but not program members.)

## The relationship

- **Benefit to the customer:** real input (not lip service) into the solution, and ultimately a solution that truly works for them.
- **Benefit to the team:** deep access to real users/customers to figure out a working solution; they test early versions and agree to **buy and serve as a public reference IF the product works for them**.

Key rules:
- **Build a general product, not a custom one.** Explain to each member that your job is a general product your company can sell to many customers — not a custom, dead-end solution for them alone (which they wouldn't want anyway). You are deeply committed to making it work extremely well for them and a handful of similar companies.
- **Find the single solution, not the union of feature requests.** Your job is not to build everything all six ask for (that yields an awful product). Dive deep with each and identify **one solution that works well for all six**.
- **Prefer no pay-in-advance.** You want a partner, not a custom-shop client; take their money after you deliver a product they love. Early-stage startups short on cash may bend this — a compromise is money in escrow.
- **Cap at eight; keep the right set.** If the problem is important you'll be overwhelmed with interest; sales may push to add more. Hold the line at no more than eight. Run a separate, essentially unlimited **early release program** for others who want the software early but aren't right for the discovery program.
- **Treat them as development partners / colleagues** — "open the kimono," help each other; relationships can last years.
- **Involve product marketing throughout** — confirm each customer has permission from their marketing org to be a public reference, and let product marketing turn references into sales tools and collateral.
- **Release before general availability.** Get the program members live and happy *before* the general release so they're ready to stand up for you at launch.

## Variations

- **Platform / API products:** like B2B, but you work with the development teams (engineers + PMs) who will use your APIs. The result is a set of reference **applications** built on your APIs, rather than reference customers.
- **Customer-enabling tools (internal):** pick **6–8 well-respected, influential internal employees** (the thought leaders others look up to — e.g., for a new customer-service-agent dashboard). They don't pay; they work closely through discovery, and once they believe it's ready they tell colleagues how much they love it.
- **Consumer products:** engage a larger group of **10–50 consumers** to get them loving the product. **Supplement** with much broader testing using people never exposed to the product. Consumers don't weigh references like business buyers, but they're influenced by social media, press, and influencers — and press writing about your product will look first for real users.

## Defining product/market fit

PMF is often "you know it when you see it" — it shows up as **happier customers, lower churn, shortened sales cycles, and rapid organic growth** — but thresholds are hard to define.

- **Sean Ellis test:** survey users in your target market who recently used the product (at least a couple of times and reached its core value per analytics) and ask how they'd feel if they could no longer use it. Choices: *very disappointed / somewhat disappointed / don't care / no longer relevant*. Rule of thumb: **>40% "very disappointed"** suggests you're at PMF. Best for **consumer** products/services.
- **For B2B:** the customer discovery program itself is the practical, effective PMF definition — **six reference customers in a specific target market = declare PMF for that market.**

PMF does not mean you're done — you keep improving the product for years. But each reference customer is a significant milestone, and reaching six in a target market (especially for B2B) is one of the most meaningful business results a product org can achieve — worth celebrating.

## Summary

This program is a lot of work, especially for the PM, but it ensures you're building a product customers love. It is **not** designed to discover the product (that's discovery work that comes next) — it gives you direct access to the target customers where you'll find the product ideas needed to generate reference customers.
