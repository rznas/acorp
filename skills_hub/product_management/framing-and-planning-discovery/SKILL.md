# Framing and Planning Discovery

Five framing and planning techniques for product discovery, from INSPIRED (Marty Cagan). Framing answers "what are we trying to accomplish and why" before any solution work. Planning organizes the work and validates demand.

## Which technique when (selector)

| Situation | Technique |
|---|---|
| Typical small/medium effort, new feature | **Opportunity Assessment** (4 questions) |
| Larger effort or redesign with multiple objectives / customer problems | **Customer Letter** (Amazon working-backward variant) |
| Brand-new product or all-new business; many risks at once | **Startup Canvas** |
| Need to plan releases, see the whole system, organize backlog | **Story Map** |
| Larger effort needing proof of value; develop references; assess PMF | **Customer Discovery Program** (see `customer-discovery-program.md`) |

These compose: frame with an Opportunity Assessment or Customer Letter (or Canvas for a new business), plan releases with a Story Map, and run a Customer Discovery Program to prove value and reach product/market fit.

## 1. Opportunity Assessment

**What it is.** An extremely simple framing technique: answer four key questions about the discovery work you are about to undertake. Saves time and grief.

**When to use.** Smaller and typically sized efforts (e.g., adding a new feature). The bare minimum framing for any discovery work.

**Steps.** The product manager answers four questions (usually takes a few minutes), then shares the answers with the product team and key stakeholders to ensure alignment:

1. **Business objective** — What objective is this work intended to address? Map it to one or more of the team's assigned objectives (e.g., growth, faster onboarding, reduce monthly churn).
2. **Key results** — How will you know if you've succeeded? Map it to at least one assigned key result, with a clear measure of success (would a 1% improvement be excellent, or a waste of time?).
3. **Customer problem** — What problem will this solve for our customers? Keep the focus on the customer. If it's for internal users, say so, but tie it back to end-customer benefit.
4. **Target market** — What type of customer are we focused on? Name the primary intended beneficiary (a user/customer persona, target market, or job to be done). Work fails when it tries to please everyone.

**Caveat.** Occasionally a senior leader requests work for strategic reasons (e.g., supporting a partnership). If infrequent, don't stress; give the team as much context as you can — these four questions may still apply.

**Output.** Four short answers, shared with team and stakeholders, that every member understands before discovery begins.

## 2. Customer Letter

**What it is.** A framing technique based on Amazon's *working backward* process, where you start an effort with a pretend press release describing the future state you want to create. The variation (refined at Nordstrom) replaces the press release with a **customer letter**: written from the perspective of a well-defined user/customer persona, sent to the CEO, explaining why the customer is so happy and grateful for the new product or redesign and how it improved their life. It includes an imagined **congratulatory CEO response to the product team** explaining how this helped the business. (A press-release version includes a customer quote instead.)

**When to use.** Larger efforts where the four-question opportunity assessment is not enough — multiple reasons, several customer problems, or multiple business objectives. The classic example is a **redesign** that must both improve the experience for current customers and perform better for new ones.

**Why it works.** It keeps the team focused on the **outcome (benefits), not the output (a feature list)**. It is primarily a framing/evangelism technique: the real readers are the product team, related/impacted teams, and leadership. If people don't see the value after reading it, the PM has more work to do — or should reconsider the effort. (It also serves as light demand validation among colleagues.) The customer-letter form is preferred over the dated press-release form because it creates more empathy for the customer's current pain.

**Steps.**
1. Pick a well-defined customer/user persona.
2. Write a letter from that satisfied future customer to the CEO: what was their pain, what changed, how their life improved — concrete benefits, not features.
3. Add the CEO's congratulatory reply to the product team explaining the business impact.
4. Share with the team, impacted teams, and leadership; use reaction as a value/evangelism gut-check.

**Output.** A short imagined customer-to-CEO letter plus CEO-to-team response, used to align and evangelize the effort.

## 3. Startup Canvas

**What it is.** A lightweight one-page framing tool (close cousins: the business model canvas and lean canvas) that calls out the key assumptions and major risks of a new product/business early, and encourages tackling the biggest risks first. Replaces old thick business plans.

**When to use.** Inventing an **entirely new product** — an early-stage startup, or an enterprise tackling an all-new business opportunity. Not for improving an existing product (where most of the canvas just duplicates your existing sales/distribution, monetization, and cost structure — use an earlier framing technique instead). Can also help a new PM build a holistic understanding of their product and business.

**Risks the canvas surfaces.** Value proposition, monetization (how you make money), go-to-market/sales, cost to produce and sell, metrics to track progress, and market size.

**The critical guidance — prioritize value risk over business-model risk.**
- It's human nature (especially for business/MBA founders) to focus on **business viability** risks they feel comfortable with: value proposition wording, pricing, channels, costs. These are real but largely *academic at this stage*.
- The biggest reason startups/new products fail is **value risk** — discovering a compelling solution customers will choose to **buy and use**. On the canvas this is *solution risk*.
- The bar is high: to make customers switch, the product must be **demonstrably and substantially better**, not just at feature parity.
- The canvas deliberately has little about the solution (to avoid premature lock-in), which tempts teams to leave the solution as "an exercise for the engineers." Resist this. **Product discovery is the startup's most important core competency.**
- Resolve the value/solution risk **first**. Don't spend time on pricing optimization, sales tools, marketing programs, and cost-cutting until you've discovered a truly valuable product. (If the market is genuinely new, use modern demand-validation techniques — they're cheap, so there's no excuse.)

**Steps.**
1. Fill the canvas to expose assumptions and risks across value, market, monetization, go-to-market, cost, and metrics.
2. Rank risks honestly; force value/solution risk to the top rather than the comfortable business-model risks.
3. Move straight into product discovery on the biggest (value) risk; defer secondary risks until value is proven.
4. Keep it lightweight — don't keep polishing the canvas to postpone discovering a solution people want to buy.

**Output.** A one-page canvas with prioritized risks, value risk first, feeding directly into discovery.

## 4. Story Map

**What it is.** A two-dimensional map of the product, introduced by Jeff Patton to fix the problem of a flat, context-free backlog of user stories. Both a framing and planning technique (also useful for ideation, prototype design, and communication).

**Structure.**
- **Horizontal axis** — major user **activities**, loosely ordered by **time** left to right (the order you'd do them, or describe them to someone).
- **Vertical axis** — progressive **detail**: each activity breaks into user tasks/stories, with **critical tasks placed higher** and **optional tasks lower**.

**When to use.** Release planning and organizing work; whenever a flat backlog lacks context. Many teams treat a high-fidelity prototype + a story map as their go-to techniques. Useful throughout both discovery and delivery.

**Steps.**
1. Lay out major user activities horizontally in time order.
2. Decompose each activity downward into tasks/stories, most critical at the top.
3. Step back for the holistic view; **draw horizontal lines to define releases/milestones** and their objectives.
4. Use the map to frame prototypes; update it as a living artifact as you learn from prototype feedback.
5. As discovery finalizes, the stories move directly into the product backlog.

**Output.** A 2D map giving every story context, a holistic view of the system over time, and clear release boundaries. Reference: *User Story Mapping*, Jeff Patton (O'Reilly, 2014).

## 5. Customer Discovery Program

**What it is.** A technique to discover and develop a set of **reference customers in parallel** with discovering and developing the product. A reference customer is a *real* customer, running your product in *production*, who *paid real money*, and is *willing to tell others* they love it. Considered the single best leading indicator of future product success — and a lot of work, mostly for the PM.

**When to use.** Larger efforts only — a new product/business, taking an existing product to a new market or geography, or a redesign. Not for features or minor projects.

**Core targets.** Develop ~6 reference customers in a **single target market** (six instills confidence, not statistical significance). Recruit **6–8** so you still land six if some drop out.

**Variations:** B2B = 6 reference customers; platform/API = 6–8 reference *applications*; internal/customer-enabling tools = 6–8 influential employees; consumer = 10–50 users (plus broader testing with fresh users).

**Product/market fit.** Six reference customers in a target market = practical, effective PMF for B2B. For consumer, use the **Sean Ellis test**: survey recent target-market users on how they'd feel if they could no longer use the product; **>40% "very disappointed"** suggests PMF.

For full recruiting criteria, the partner relationship, single-solution rule, all variation details, and the PMF/Sean Ellis test, see `customer-discovery-program.md`.

**Output.** ~6 happy, paying, public reference customers in one target market — the best sales/marketing tool and the milestone that declares product/market fit for that market.
