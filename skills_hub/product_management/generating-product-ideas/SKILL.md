# Generating Product Ideas

Four ideation techniques for producing high-quality, relevant product ideas
that help solve a real business or customer problem. From INSPIRED by Marty
Cagan (Part: Discovery Ideation Techniques).

## Core mindset

- Good teams ideate themselves; they are given business *problems* to solve,
  not prioritized solutions on a roadmap. Most roadmap items (from big
  customers, stakeholders, or execs) are rarely the quality of idea you want.
- If a team has a real problem to solve and interacts directly and frequently
  with actual users and customers, getting enough good ideas is not a problem.
- Caveat: an exciting idea is not a green light to build. You still must test
  it for value, usability, feasibility, and business viability.

## Technique selector

- Want deep customer empathy (and ideas as a by-product) -> **Concierge Test**
- Want fresh ideas from engineers + build missionary culture -> **Hack Day**
- Want ongoing learning and a steady idea source -> **Customer Interviews**
- Want big, unanticipated opportunities -> watch for **Customer Misbehavior**

Bring three people to interviews and concierge tests: product manager,
product designer, and one engineer (shared learning).

---

## 1. Customer Interviews

**What:** The most basic and most powerful PM skill. A family of techniques
(informal "get out of the building" chats through formal contextual inquiry)
for understanding customers and uncovering breakthrough product ideas.

**When:** Always. Establish it as a permanent, recurring habit, not a
one-off. It is also a prerequisite for later qualitative testing techniques.

**The four questions to always answer:**
1. Are your customers who you think they are?
2. Do they really have the problems you think they have?
3. How does the customer solve this problem today?
4. What would be required for them to switch?

**Steps / tips:**
- **Frequency:** Minimum of 2-3 hours of interviews per week, every week.
- **Purpose:** Go to *learn*, not to *prove* anything. This mindset must be
  sincere.
- **Recruit:** Primarily people in your intended target market; ask for ~1 hour.
- **Location:** Best to observe customers in their native environment, there is
  much to learn from their surroundings. A convenient location or your office
  is fine; video call is weaker but far better than not doing it.
- **Preparation:** Be clear which problem you think they have and how you will
  confirm or contradict that.
- **Who attends:** PM, designer, and a rotating engineer. Designer usually
  drives, PM takes notes, engineer observes.
- **Interview:** Keep it natural and informal; ask open-ended questions; learn
  what they do *today* (not just what they wish they did).
- **Afterward:** Debrief together to confirm you heard the same things, and
  keep any promises made to the customer.
- **Bonus:** After the four questions are answered, use the time to try out
  current product ideas with the customer.

## 2. Concierge Test

**What:** You personally do the customer's job for them, manually. Like a hotel
concierge fetching theater tickets, *you* become the concierge and perform the
tasks the user would perform.

**When:** To quickly generate high-quality ideas while building deep customer
understanding and empathy that motivates the team and drives strong solutions.

**Steps:**
- Go out to actual users/customers and ask them to show you how they work.
- Ask them to train you, then do their job in their shoes.
- For a customer-enabling product, the "users" may be your own colleagues; the
  technique is identical, ask them to teach you their job.
- Do it as a trio: PM, designer, and one engineer (shared learning).

**Note:** Sitting with customer service/success is also a good idea source, but
that is helping customers after they hit a problem, not the same as doing their
job alongside them.

## 3. The Power of Customer (and Developer) Misbehavior

**What:** A third way to find opportunities (beyond following the market or
following the technology): allow, and even encourage, customers to use your
product to solve problems you never planned for or officially support.

**When:** When you spot customers using the product in unintended ways, treat it
as valuable signal rather than a support nuisance. Especially powerful for
marketplaces and platforms.

**Steps:**
- Monitor what customers *want* to do, not just what you designed for. Provide
  an open path for the unexpected (e.g., eBay's "Everything Else" category).
- When you find unintended use, dig in: learn what problem they are solving and
  why they think your product is the right foundation.
- Look for patterns across many such cases; they reveal big product
  opportunities. (eBay invested in safe car buying/transport only *after*
  customer demand emerged, becoming one of the largest used-car sellers.)
- Don't reflexively shut it down over support obligations; the special case can
  be strategic and worth supporting.

**Developer misbehavior (top innovation source):**
- Expose product services via public APIs: tell developers "here is what we can
  do, build something amazing we couldn't anticipate" (e.g., Facebook opening
  its social graph).
- Developers are one of the most consistently best sources of truly innovative
  ideas, because they see what is *just now* possible.

## 4. Hack Days

**What:** Self-organizing groups from the product teams explore ideas and build
prototypes that can be evaluated and, if promising, tested on real users.

**When:** To quickly surface a range of high-potential ideas and to pull
engineers into ideation.

**Two types:**
- **Undirected:** People explore any product ideas they like, as long as it is
  at least loosely related to the company's mission.
- **Directed:** Tied to a specific customer problem (e.g., "too hard to learn")
  or business objective (e.g., "reduce churn," "increase lifetime value"); teams
  self-organize on any idea that might address it.

**Two key benefits:**
- **Practical:** Forces inclusion of engineers at ideation. Many of the best
  ideas come from engineers, this guarantees that channel is open.
- **Cultural:** Builds a team of *missionaries, not mercenaries*. Engineers dive
  deeper into business context and take a larger role in innovation.

**Steps:**
- Pick directed or undirected based on whether you have a specific problem/goal.
- Let teams self-organize around ideas they care about.
- Require a prototype output that can be evaluated and tested.
